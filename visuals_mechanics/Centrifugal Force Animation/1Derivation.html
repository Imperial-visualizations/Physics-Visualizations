Fixed in rotating frame

Vector has no angular speed in the rotating frame \(\mathbf{V_{Rotating}} = 0\)

Using the standard equation \(v = \omega r\) \(V_{Inertial} = \rho \omega = r \sin \theta * \omega = \omega r \sin \theta\)

\(\mathbf{V_{Inertial}} = \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}\)

Moving in rotating frame

$$\mathbf{V_{Rotating}} \neq 0$$
This means there are now two contributions to the velocity in the inertial frame: one from the vector rotating in the frame and one from the frame itself rotating relative to the inertial frame. $$\mathbf{V_{I}} = \mathbf{V_{R}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}$$ where I and R refer to the inertial and rotating frames respectively.

This is a general result and (written in terms of derivatives) can relate any vector in the inertial frame to one in the rotating frame: $$\frac{d\boldsymbol{r}}{dt}|_{I} = \frac{d\boldsymbol{r}}{dt}|_{R} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}$$
which reduces to formula for a fixed point in rotating frame if \(\frac{d\boldsymbol{r}}{dt}|_{R} = 0\).

Accelerating in rotating frame

\(\mathbf{a|_{I}} = \frac{d\mathbf{V_{I}}}{dt}|_{I} = \frac{d}{dt} (\mathbf{V_R} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})|_{I} \)
using the expression relating speeds between the frames. $$ = \color{hsl(200, 80%, 50%)}{\frac{d}{dt}(\boldsymbol{V_{R}})|_{I}} + \color{hsl(20,80%,50%}{\frac{d}{dt}(\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})|_{I}}$$ Using the formula given previously relating derivatives in the rotating and inertial frames: $$ = \color{hsl(200, 80%, 50%)}{\frac{d}{dt}(\boldsymbol{V_{R}})|_{R} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{V_{R}}} + \color{hsl(20,80%,50%}{\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \frac{d\boldsymbol{r}}{dt}|_{I} + \frac{d \boldsymbol{\omega}}{dt}|_{I} \times \mathbf{r}}$$ $$ = \color{hsl(200, 80%, 50%)}{\mathbf{a_{R}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{V_{R}}} + \color{hsl(20,80%,50%}{\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{V_{I}} + \boldsymbol{\dot{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}}$$ Once again using the formula relating the velocities, we can substitute \(\mathbf{V_{I}}\) for \(\mathbf{V_{R}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \boldsymbol{r}\) $$ = \color{hsl(200, 80%, 50%)}{\mathbf{a_{R}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{V_{R}}} + \color{hsl(20,80%,50%)}{\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\mathbf{V_{R}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}) + \boldsymbol{\dot{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}}$$ $$ = \mathbf{a_{R}} + 2\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{V_{R}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}) + \boldsymbol{\dot{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}$$ where the brackets are necessary otherwise \(\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \boldsymbol{\omega} = 0\).
Using \(\mathbf{F} = m \mathbf{a}\) and the relation above, we find that the force in the rotating frame is the sum of the force in the inertial frame and 3 other inertial forces. $$ \mathbf{F_{R}} = \mathbf{F_{I}} \color{green}{-2m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{V_{R}}} \color{blue}{-m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})} \color{red}{-m\boldsymbol{\dot{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}}$$ and these are the forces we give names to, being labelled as: $$ \mathbf{F_{Rotating}} = {\mathbf{F_{Inertial}}} + \color{green}{\mathbf{F_{Coriolis}}} + \color{blue}{\mathbf{F_{Centrifugal}}} + \color{red}{\mathbf{F_{Euler}}} $$
