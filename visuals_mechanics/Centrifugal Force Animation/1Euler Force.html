The Euler Force (given by \(-m\boldsymbol{\dot{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}\)) is the first inertial force we look at. It is rather unique because it depends on the angular acceleration rather than speed .

\(\boldsymbol{\dot{\omega}}\) doesn't change much for the Earth (the length of a day can be considered constant) and so for us the Euler force is not important.We will neglect the Euler force and focus on the other two which dominate.

It is still important to recognise it exists and its relation to the other forces. Looking back at the expressions for the forces (click on the force name to show the direction)

Centrifugal Force: \(-m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}\))
Coriolis Force: \(-2m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{v}\)
Euler Force: \(-m\boldsymbol{\dot{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}\)

tells us that Centrifugal and Euler force will always be perpendicular to each other (since \(\boldsymbol{\dot{\omega}}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\omega}\) are parallel) and the direction relative to Coriolis force depends on velocity in the rotating frame.

Note:

  • \(\mathbf{v}\) is in the same direction as \(\mathbf{r}\)
  • The rotating frame is decelerating
  • Remember how the negative sign affects the direction of forces
